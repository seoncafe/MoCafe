\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This note collects the physics and the numerical algorithms behind MoCafe, the
Monte-Carlo dust radiative-transfer code: photon transport and the peeling-off
estimator, panchromatic (SED) transport by grey rescaling, the Lucy~(1999)
mean-intensity estimator, the two dust-emission solvers (Lucy$+$SEDust and
Bjorkman \& Wood), the Modified Random Walk, the galaxy source models, and the
random-sampling routines (alias method, exponential disk, deprojected Sersic).
It is a companion to the user guide, which covers installation, the input
reference, and the outputs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Monte-Carlo radiative transfer}
%=============================================================================

MoCafe propagates photon \emph{packets} (weighted bundles of photons) through a
dusty medium.  A packet carries a position, a propagation direction, a weight
$w$, a wavelength bin, and---in SED/emission mode---a physical luminosity
$L_{\rm p}$ [erg\,s$^{-1}$].  The medium is described by an extinction
coefficient $\kext=\ksca+\kabs$ (per unit length), a single-scattering albedo
$a=\ksca/\kext$, and a scattering phase function.

\subsection{Free path and optical depth}
The probability that a packet travels an optical depth $\tau$ before an
interaction is $e^{-\tau}$, so a free path is drawn as
\begin{equation}
  \tau = -\ln(1-\xi), \qquad \xi \in [0,1),
\end{equation}
and converted to a physical length by integrating $\kext$ along the ray
(Amanatides--Woo voxel traversal on the Cartesian grid, a ray--sphere plus DDA
walk in the clumpy medium, and an octree neighbour walk on the AMR grid).  The
three ray tracers share one interface so scattering and peeling-off are
grid-agnostic.

\subsection{Forced first scattering and reduced weight}
For optically thin media most packets would escape without interacting, wasting
samples.  With \pp{use\_reduced\_wgt} the first interaction is \emph{forced}
inside the grid.  If $\tau_0$ is the total optical depth from the birth point to
the far edge, the first free path is drawn from the \emph{truncated}
distribution
\begin{equation}
  \tau = -\ln\!\big[\,1-\xi\,(1-e^{-\tau_0})\big],
\end{equation}
and the packet weight is reduced by the probability that it would have interacted
at all,
\begin{equation}
  w \leftarrow w\,(1-e^{-\tau_0}).
\end{equation}
The escaping fraction $e^{-\tau_0}$ is accounted for by the direct (unscattered)
peel-off, so no energy is lost.  The $n_{\rm scatt}=0$ step is special-cased in
the transport loop for this reason.

\subsection{Peeling-off estimator}
Rather than waiting for packets to reach an observer by chance, at every
emission and scattering site MoCafe adds the \emph{expected} contribution toward
each observer (the ``peeling-off'' or next-event estimator).  For a scattering
at position $\mathbf r$ with incoming direction $\hat{\mathbf k}$, the
contribution to observer $o$ in direction $\hat{\mathbf n}_o$ at distance $d_o$
is
\begin{equation}
  \Delta I_o = \frac{w\,a\,\Phi(\hat{\mathbf k}\!\cdot\!\hat{\mathbf n}_o)}{4\pi}\,
               e^{-\tau_o}\, \frac{1}{d_o^2},
  \label{eq:peel}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi$ is the (normalized) phase function and $\tau_o$ is the optical depth
from $\mathbf r$ to the observer along $\hat{\mathbf n}_o$.  For an internal
all-sky observer (\S\ref{sec:allsky}) the same quantity is binned into the
HEALPix pixel of the arrival direction instead of a tangent-plane image.  The
peel is computed through the \code{raytrace\_to\_edge} procedure pointer so that
the Cartesian, clumpy, and AMR media reuse identical scattering/peeling code.

%=============================================================================
\section{Scattering}
%=============================================================================
The default phase function is Henyey--Greenstein with asymmetry $g$,
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_{\rm HG}(\mu) = \frac{1-g^2}{2\,(1+g^2-2g\mu)^{3/2}}, \qquad \mu=\cos\theta,
\end{equation}
sampled analytically for the scattering angle.  When a Mueller matrix is
supplied (\pp{scatt\_mat\_file}) the code carries the Stokes vector
$(I,Q,U,V)$, samples $\cos\theta$ from the tabulated $S_{11}$ with an alias
table (\S\ref{sec:alias}), and rotates/updates the Stokes vector through the
scattering matrix.  In that case the peel-off of Eq.~\eqref{eq:peel} uses the
matrix-weighted phase function.

%=============================================================================
\section{Panchromatic (SED) transport}\label{sec:sed}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Grey rescaling}
In SED mode a packet is assigned a wavelength bin $\lambda$ at birth and keeps it
until absorption.  Instead of storing a separate opacity grid per wavelength,
MoCafe stores the density (hence the optical-depth structure) at a single
reference wavelength $\lambda_{\rm ref}$ and rescales per packet
(Jonsson~2006-style),
\begin{equation}
  s_{\rm ext}(\lambda) = \frac{\kext(\lambda)}{\kext(\lambda_{\rm ref})},
  \qquad
  \tau(\lambda) = s_{\rm ext}(\lambda)\,\tau_{\rm ref}.
\end{equation}
The ray tracers integrate $\tau_{\rm ref}$ and multiply by the packet's
$s_{\rm ext}$, so all three tracers are shared with the monochromatic path and
memory does not grow with the number of wavelengths.  The wavelength-dependent
albedo $a(\lambda)$ and asymmetry $g(\lambda)$ come from the same
\code{kext\_file} table ($\lambda$, $a$, $\langle\cos\rangle$, $C_{\rm ext}/$H).

\subsection{Wavelength sampling}
The source spectrum $L_\lambda$ (a Planck function at \pp{tstar}, a tabulated
\pp{source\_spectrum}, or per component in the multi-source case) is turned into
a luminosity fraction per bin and sampled with an alias table.  Each packet
then carries a physical luminosity $L_{\rm p}=L_{\rm tot}/N_{\rm phot}$, which
makes the emission energy budget absolute (erg\,s$^{-1}$).

%=============================================================================
\section{Mean intensity in each cell (Lucy 1999)}\label{sec:jlam}
%=============================================================================
The heating of the dust is set by the mean intensity $\Jlam$ in each cell.
MoCafe uses the Lucy~(1999) \emph{pathlength} estimator, which accumulates a
contribution from every path segment a packet takes through a cell, not just
from discrete interaction points:
\begin{equation}
  \Jlam = \frac{1}{4\pi\,V\,\Delta\lambda\,d_{\rm cm}^2}
          \sum_{\text{segments in cell}} L_{\rm p}\, w\, \ell,
  \label{eq:jlucy}
\end{equation}
where $\ell$ is the length of the segment, $V$ the cell volume, $\Delta\lambda$
the bin width, and $d_{\rm cm}$ the length unit in cm.  Because every segment
contributes, the estimator has far lower variance than a collision estimator,
especially in optically thin cells.  The absorbed power in a cell follows as
\begin{equation}
  P_{\rm abs} = 4\pi V \int \kabs(\lambda)\,\Jlam \, d\lambda,
\end{equation}
and is written to the \code{\_jlam} output together with a consistency check
(the pathlength tally \code{EABS\_A} versus an event counter \code{EABS\_B}).
The forced-first-flight segment is added analytically.

%=============================================================================
\section{Dust thermal emission}
%=============================================================================
Two solvers turn the absorbed energy into an emission spectrum.  Both conserve
energy exactly: the total emitted luminosity equals the total absorbed
luminosity.

\subsection{Mode 1: Lucy (1999) with SEDust}\label{sec:lucy}
The radiation field $\Jlam$ from Eq.~\eqref{eq:jlucy} is handed cell-by-cell to
the SEDust grain library, which returns the emission spectrum
$\varepsilon_\lambda$ for the local field.  SEDust integrates over the grain-size
distribution and, for small grains and PAHs, over the \emph{temperature
probability distribution} $P(T)$ produced by single-photon (stochastic) heating,
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon_\lambda = \int da\, \frac{dn}{da}\,\pi a^2 Q_{\rm abs}(a,\lambda)
        \int dT\, P(a,T)\, \pi B_\lambda(T),
\end{equation}
plus an equilibrium term for large grains.  The emission in each cell is then
normalized so that its integral equals the locally absorbed power, which makes
energy conservation exact regardless of the SEDust internal normalization.  The
emitted spectrum is resampled onto the transport grid, and the emission is
either integrated directly (the intrinsic SED) or transported as a second batch
of packets to form the emergent SED and the pixel-resolved emission image.

\paragraph{Speed options.}  The exact stochastic solve is $\mathcal O(0.1\,$s$)$
per cell.  \pp{dust\_single\_teq} replaces $P(T)$ by a single mixture
equilibrium temperature (fast, no PAH/stochastic features).
\pp{dust\_fast\_table} builds a reference emission table over the field
intensity $U=\int\Jlam d\lambda / \int J_\lambda^{\rm ref} d\lambda$ once, then
interpolates each cell in $U$ ($\sim$1\% SED-shape error, keeps PAH/stochastic).

\paragraph{Iteration.}  With \pp{dust\_niter}$>1$ the emitted packets are
transported so that dust self-absorption heats other cells; the field
$\Jlam \to \Jlam^{\rm stellar}+\Jlam^{\rm dust}$ and the emission is recomputed
until the total emitted luminosity converges to \pp{dust\_tol}.  This matters
only at high infrared optical depth.

\paragraph{Grain models.}  \pp{dust\_model\_sed} selects \code{astrodust}
(Hensley \& Draine 2023), \code{dl07} (Draine \& Li 2007), or \code{zubko}
(Zubko et al.\ 2004 BARE-GR-S).

\subsection{Mode 2: Bjorkman \& Wood (2001)}\label{sec:bw}
The immediate-reemission scheme uses fixed-energy packets and a mixture
mean opacity.  When a packet is absorbed in a cell, the cell's absorbed energy
increases, raising its equilibrium temperature $T$; the packet is re-emitted at
once with a wavelength drawn from the \emph{temperature-correction} spectrum
\begin{equation}
  \frac{dP}{d\lambda} \propto \kabs(\lambda)\,
      \frac{\partial B_\lambda(T)}{\partial T},
\end{equation}
which corrects the previously-emitted spectrum for the temperature increment so
that, in steady state, the cell radiates a $\kabs B_\lambda(T)$ spectrum.  No
iteration and no radiation-field storage are needed; the method is approximate
(equilibrium, mixture opacity, no PAH/stochastic features) but cheap.  The
emergent dust light lands directly in the observer \code{Scattered} SED.

\section{Modified Random Walk}\label{sec:mrw}
%=============================================================================
In cells that are very optically thick to \emph{scattering} a packet takes many
tiny steps.  The Modified Random Walk (Min et al.\ 2009; Robitaille 2010)
replaces them with a single diffusion step.  A sphere of radius $R_0$ (the
distance to the nearest cell wall) is inscribed; the packet is moved to a random
point on that sphere, and the physical path length travelled inside is sampled
from the tabulated diffusion first-passage distribution $\zeta(y)$.  MoCafe
diffuses with the \emph{scattering} opacity and decays the weight by the
absorption along the path, $w\leftarrow w\,e^{-a_{\rm abs}\,c t}$, and applies
Russian roulette (survival probability \code{RR\_SURV} below a cutoff
\code{RR\_CUT}) so that high-albedo, high-$\tau$ clouds terminate.  MRW triggers
when the nearest-wall scattering optical depth exceeds \pp{mrw\_gamma}; its
speed-up is large only for near-pure-scattering, very thick cells.

%=============================================================================
\section{Source (galaxy) models}\label{sec:galaxy}
%=============================================================================
A stellar component emits packets from a spatial distribution.  Multiple
components (\pp{nsource}) are chosen in proportion to luminosity.

\subsection{Exponential disk}
A razor-thin exponential disk has surface density $\Sigma(r)\propto e^{-r/r_s}$,
so the probability of a packet at cylindrical radius $r$ is
$p(r)\propto 2\pi r\,\Sigma(r)\propto r\,e^{-r/r_s}$.  This is the
$\mathrm{Gamma}(2)$ (double-exponential) law, sampled exactly by \code{rand\_r1exp}
(the sum of two exponential deviates).  The vertical structure is exponential
(\code{rand\_zexp}) or $\mathrm{sech}^2$ (\code{rand\_sech2}).

\subsection{Spiral arms}
Log-spiral arms multiply the disk by
\begin{equation}
  1 + A\,\sin\!\Big[m\Big(\frac{\ln r}{\tan p}-\phi\Big)\Big],
\end{equation}
with $m$ arms, pitch angle $p$, and amplitude $A$.  For \emph{sources} the
azimuth is drawn by rejection against this factor (accept if
$(1+A)\,\xi \le 1+A\sin[\dots]$); for the \emph{dust} density the cell opacity
is multiplied by the same factor.  Seen face-on, the absorbed-power map traces
the arms (validated in \file{examples/galaxy/spiral\_faceon.in}).

\subsection{Sersic bulge (deprojection + alias sampling)}\label{sec:sersic}
The projected 2-D Sersic profile is
$I(R)=I_0\exp[-b_m(R/R_e)^{1/m}]$, with $b_m$ from Ciotti \& Bertin~(1999).
MoCafe emits from the \emph{3-D} luminosity distribution, obtained by
deprojecting $I(R)$ with an Abel transform; the deprojected cumulative
luminosity within spherical radius $r$ is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal L(<r) = \gamma\!\big(2m{+}1,\,b_m r^{1/m}\big)
     + \frac{2}{\pi}\frac{b_m^{2m+1}}{m\,\Gamma(2m{+}1)}\,r^2
       \!\int_1^\infty\!\! x\,f(x)\,e^{-b_m(rx)^{1/m}}(rx)^{1/m}\,dx,
\end{equation}
computed once on a log-radius grid (\code{sersic\_cumulative\_3D}).  The radius
is then drawn with the \emph{alias} method (\S\ref{sec:alias}) rather than an
inverse-CDF search: the bin probabilities (differences of the cumulative)
build an alias table, and \code{rand\_alias\_linear} returns a continuous radius
by linear interpolation within the selected bin.  The point is placed
isotropically and flattened in $z$ by the axial ratio.  The alias-sampled
distribution reproduces the analytic cumulative to $\sim10^{-4}$.

\subsection{Boxy / bar / x-bar bulges}
Generalized-ellipsoid bulges use the radius
$r=(|x|^n+|y|^n+|z|^n)^{1/n}$ with boxiness $n$ ($n=2$ ellipsoid, $n>2$ boxy)
and a power-law (\code{boxy}), Gaussian (\code{bar}), or peanut (\code{xbar})
profile.  These are drawn with a slice-within-Gibbs sampler
(\code{random\_bulge.f90}): an auxiliary level is drawn under the current
density and each coordinate is updated uniformly on the resulting slice.
Consecutive draws are therefore correlated---adequate for a bulge, but
statistically weaker than the independent alias-sampled Sersic.

%=============================================================================
\section{Random sampling}\label{sec:alias}
%=============================================================================
\subsection{Alias method}
Walker's alias method samples an index from a discrete distribution
$\{p_k\}$ in $\mathcal O(1)$.  A setup pass (\code{random\_alias\_setup})
converts $\{p_k\}$ into an acceptance probability $q_k$ and an alias $A_k$ per
bin; a draw picks a random bin $k$ and returns $k$ with probability $q_k$, else
$A_k$.  MoCafe uses it for wavelength sampling, the Mueller $S_{11}$, and the
Sersic radius.

\subsection{Continuous alias (piecewise-linear)}
For a continuous variable tabulated at nodes $x_i$ with density $p_i$, the bin
selection probability is the trapezoid area
$\tfrac12(p_i+p_{i+1})(x_{i+1}-x_i)$; after the alias picks a bin, the value is
returned by inverting the linear density inside it
(\code{rand\_alias\_linear}).  This gives an independent, continuous draw and is
used for external-illumination angles, the scattering angle, and the Sersic
radius.

%=============================================================================
\section{HEALPix all-sky interior observer}\label{sec:allsky}
%=============================================================================
For an observer \emph{inside} the medium (the Milky-Way case) there is no single
image plane.  Each scattering / dust-reemission / direct event peels toward the
observer point, and the contribution is binned into the HEALPix pixel (RING
scheme, $n_{\rm pix}=12\,n_{\rm side}^2$, equal-area pixels
$\Omega=4\pi/n_{\rm pix}$) of the arrival direction $\hat{\mathbf n}$, computed
by \code{vec2pix}.  The optical depth to the observer is integrated along the
matching ray (Cartesian or AMR).  The output carries the standard HEALPix
keywords so it reads directly with \code{healpy}.  Because each event maps to
the single pixel of its \emph{source} direction, a diffuse map needs many events
distributed over the sky; concentrated (inner-disk) far-infrared emission is
sparse at high $n_{\rm side}$ and benefits from a finer spatial grid or
smoothing.

%=============================================================================
\section{Energy conservation}
%=============================================================================
Energy conservation is the main internal check.  The total stellar luminosity
equals the escaped starlight plus the absorbed (hence re-emitted) power; the
dust solvers normalize the emission to the absorbed power cell by cell, so the
integrated intrinsic dust luminosity equals \code{L\_ABS}, and the emergent SED
integrated over $4\pi d^2$ recovers the same value to Monte-Carlo accuracy.  The
\code{\_jlam} output additionally reports the pathlength-tallied absorbed power
against an independent event counter.
